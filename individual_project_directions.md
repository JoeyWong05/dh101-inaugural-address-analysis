# DIGHUM101 – Individual Coding Project Directions

## Overview

The individual coding project is an opportunity to become more comfortable with Python. The scale and degree of individual coding projects will vary. If you already have some programming experience, you may want to develop a larger, more intensive project. If you’re new to coding, your project may borrow code and ideas from class and apply them to different data or variables. In either case, the goal is simply to practice your coding skills and develop greater familiarity with Python.

It is completely fine if parts of your initial plans for the individual coding project prove more difficult or complicated than you anticipated. While you will need to submit executable code as part of the project, it is also important to _document and describe what didn’t work_. What proved too time-consuming? What parts of coding in Python were challenging? What aspects of your chosen method proved difficult to implement or understand? These details should be included in your submissions alongside your final code versions.

The Individual Coding Project is worth 40% of your overall grade. It is split into three parts: the Topic Statement, the Demonstration of Code, and the Project Showcase. 

## Topic Statement

The topic statement is a 2-page, single-spaced document that describes the following:

1. The computational approach(es) you intend to implement with Python in your project. These may be methods we discuss in class–anything from word frequency analysis to geospatial analysis to topic modeling to text classification. Methods not addressed in class may also be viable, but you should discuss them with your instructor to ensure they fall within common practices in the digital humanities.
2. The data you intend to use in your project. We will discuss datasets in class. You may work with a dataset provided through the course. If you plan to curate or scrape your own dataset, please be mindful of the time and labor it may take to compile.
3. Research question(s) you intend to explore with Python and data. These should be properly scoped to your chosen dataset and methods (and the time restrictions of a 6-week class).

__The due date is Friday, June 5th__ at 11:59PM. __It is 10% of your overall grade.__ You must submit your topic statement as a pdf via BCourse. 

## Demonstration of Code

The demonstration of code is a Jupyter Notebook that demonstrates the following:

- How and where to access your data for the project. The data may be files already part of the of course materials. Or it may come from somewhere else. In any case, your notebook needs to explain how to access the data. If you’re keeping your data on your local device, you’ll need to find a way to host it publicly. We will discuss this in greater detail in class. 
- Executable code that demonstrates your intended method. Alongside this code, you’ll need to draft explanatory text that describes your research questions and clarifies how the code seeks to answer them.
- A brief reflection section, describing what worked, what didn’t, and what you learned in the process.

You must host the demonstration of code on GitHub. __The due date is Sunday, June 28th__ at 11:59pm. __It is worth 20% of your overall grade.__ You must submit a link to your GitHub Repository via BCourse.

## Project Showcase

The project showcase is a low-stakes way to share your coding project with the class. It is a 5-minute, pre-recorded presentation of your demonstration of code. In the pre-recorded video, you must walk viewers through your notebook and explain your project.

This video will be uploaded to BCourse. __It is due Monday, June 29th__ at noon. You are also required to watch your classmates’ showcases and comment on at least two of them by __Tuesday, June 30th by 11:59pm.__ The project showcase is worth __10% of your overall grade.__
